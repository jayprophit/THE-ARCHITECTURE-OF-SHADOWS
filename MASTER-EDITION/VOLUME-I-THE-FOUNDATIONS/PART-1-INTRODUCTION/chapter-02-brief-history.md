# Chapter 2: A Brief History of Drugs

## Ancient Use (Opium, Cannabis, Coca)

The use of psychoactive substances dates back to prehistoric times. Archaeological evidence suggests opium use in Mesopotamia as early as 4,000 BCE, cannabis cultivation in China around 2,700 BCE, and coca leaf use in the Andes for over 8,000 years.

## Classical Civilisations (Greece, Rome, Egypt)

Ancient Greeks used opium for medicinal and recreational purposes. The Egyptians documented the use of various plant medicines in the Ebers Papyrus (c. 1,550 BCE). Roman society incorporated opium into religious and social practices.

## The Islamic Golden Age and Preservation of Knowledge

During the Islamic Golden Age (8th-14th centuries), Arab scholars preserved and expanded upon Greek and Roman medical knowledge, including the use of opium and other medicinal substances.

## The Age of Exploration and Colonial Trade

European exploration brought new substances to global markets. The opium trade became a major economic force, with colonial powers establishing monopolies on production and distribution.

## The 19th Century: Morphine, Cocaine, and Patent Medicines

The 19th century saw the isolation of active compounds: morphine (1804), cocaine (1860), and heroin (1874). These were widely available in patent medicines and tonics.

## The 20th Century: Prohibition and the "War on Drugs"

The 20th century brought increasing regulation and prohibition. The Harrison Narcotics Tax Act (1914) in the US began the era of criminalisation, culminating in the "War on Drugs" declared by President Nixon in 1971.

## The Modern Era: Synthetic Drugs and Globalisation

The late 20th and early 21st centuries have seen the rise of synthetic drugs, global trafficking networks, and the emergence of new psychoactive substances designed to circumvent legal restrictions.

## Drugs and Countercultural Movements

- **Beat Generation (1940s-1960s)**: Exploration of consciousness through substances
- **Hippies (1960s)**: Psychedelic revolution and social change
- **Acid House (1988-1989)**: Second Summer of Love in the UK
- **Rave Culture (1990s)**: Electronic music and MDMA culture

## 5,000-Year History of Opium

Opium has transitioned from a revered medicinal substance to a controlled narcotic. Its history reflects changing social attitudes, medical understanding, and political responses to drug use.
