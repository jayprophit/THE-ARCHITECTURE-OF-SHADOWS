# Chapter 4: The "War on Drugs"

## Origins and Evolution (Nixon, Reagan, Global Expansion)

The "War on Drugs" was officially declared by President Richard Nixon in 1971, though drug prohibition policies existed earlier. President Ronald Reagan dramatically expanded these policies in the 1980s with increased funding, mandatory minimum sentences, and militarised enforcement. The approach was subsequently adopted globally through US pressure and international treaties.

## Key Figures and Policies

- **Richard Nixon**: Declared the "War on Drugs," established the DEA (1973)
- **Ronald Reagan**: Expanded enforcement, "Just Say No" campaign, crack epidemic response
- **George H.W. Bush**: Continued escalation, increased overseas interdiction
- **Bill Clinton**: Maintained harsh policies despite rhetoric of treatment
- **International Treaties**: UN Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961), Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971)

## Consequences and Failures

The War on Drugs has produced numerous unintended consequences:
- Mass incarceration, particularly affecting minority communities
- Growth of powerful criminal organisations
- Public health crises rather than reduced drug use
- Erosion of civil liberties
- Corruption and violence in producer nations
- Trillions of dollars spent with minimal impact on supply

## The Human Cost of Prohibition

Prohibition has resulted in:
- Over 1 million drug-related deaths annually worldwide
- Millions incarcerated for non-violent drug offenses
- Families destroyed by incarceration and addiction
- Communities ravaged by violence and policing
- Stigma preventing people from seeking help
- Barriers to harm reduction and treatment
